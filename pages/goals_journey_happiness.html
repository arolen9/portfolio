On Happines, Means & Ends

The question of how to choose goals to maximize happiness during the journey is a core theme in several philosophical traditions.

Summary: Goals that maximize happiness on the journey

How to choose goals to maximize happiness while working on them:

  • Epicureanism: Choose simple, easily controllable goals and focus on the quiet pleasures of life and friendship.

  • Stoicism: Choose goals based on developing an internal virtuous character rather than on external, uncontrollable outcomes.

  • Modern Eudaimonism: Choose goals that align with your deepest values and strengths, and which provide opportunities for personal growth and contribution to others

Epicureanism: The pursuit of pleasure

Epicurus (c. 341â€“270 BCE) is a key figure who directly addresses the goal of maximizing happiness. His philosophy, Epicureanism, is often misunderstood as simple hedonism. While he did identify pleasure (hedonia) as the highest good, his idea of pleasure was not about overindulgence but about tranquility and the absence of pain. 

  • Defining pleasure: For Epicurus, the truest form of happiness is ataraxia (freedom from fear and mental disturbance) and aponia (the absence of physical pain).

  • Maximizing happiness: He would advise choosing goals that are modest and easily attainable to avoid the anxiety and pain of striving for things that are difficult to control, such as wealth, fame, or power.

  • Simple goals: Epicurean goals focus on cultivating simple pleasures and, most importantly, on friendship and the quiet appreciation of life. Choosing goals related to inner peace rather than external outcomes is key to minimizing frustration. 

Stoicism: Virtue as the only good

The Stoics, such as Epictetus and Seneca, focused on inner control and resilience rather than the pursuit of happiness itself. While they sought eudaimonia (flourishing), they saw it as a byproduct of virtue, not a direct goal. 

  • Goal choice: Stoics advise choosing goals that are fully within your control and aligned with virtue. A Stoic musician would focus on writing the best music they could, not on achieving a number-one hit, as the latter is not fully under their control.

  • Accepting hardship: This approach doesn't guarantee a happy journey in the modern sense but promises a peaceful and resilient one. A person who practices Stoicism can endure misfortunes with equanimity because their goals are internal (moral character) rather than external (circumstances). 

Modern Eudaimonism: Meaningful growth

Contemporary virtue ethics and positive psychology have evolved the Aristotelian concept of eudaimonia to suggest that pursuing goals aligned with one's values and strengths is a path to happiness. This involves a more nuanced approach to goal selection.

  • The path to flow: Psychologists have found that people report the greatest happiness when they are engaged in challenging activities that require their full attention, a state known as "flow".

  • Meaning vs. pleasure: Research distinguishes between hedonic happiness (short-term pleasure) and eudaimonic happiness (long-term satisfaction from meaning and purpose). Studies have shown that pursuing goals related to personal growth, meaningful work, and contributing to the well-being of others leads to more robust life satisfaction than purely hedonistic goals.

  • Balance of goals: The modern approach suggests that a mix of goals is ideal. While a person should pursue a larger, meaningful mission (eudaimonic), they should also incorporate smaller, more manageable goals that provide regular, achievable rewards to sustain motivation and happiness along the way. 

Summary: Goals that maximize happiness on the journey

To maximize happiness while on the journey, different philosophical traditions suggest choosing goals in the following ways:

  • Epicureanism: Choose simple, easily controllable goals and focus on the quiet pleasures of life and friendship.

  • Stoicism: Choose goals based on developing an internal virtuous character rather than on external, uncontrollable outcomes.

  • Modern Eudaimonism: Choose goals that align with your deepest values and strengths, and which provide opportunities for personal growth and contribution to others
